Most importat predictors:

6.4.a

The most important variable for price prediction seem to be age, mileage, horsepower and automatic airco. That is because their are statistically significant and the absolute values of their coefficients are larger than other variables’ coefficients. It followsw that their p-values are also ver small. The two dummies for Petrol and Diesel have large coefficient, respectively 0.362 and 0.584 , but the Petrol one is not as significant.

  • The Age variable with a coefficient of -0.541
  • The Mileage coefficient amounts to -0.185
  • The Automatic air-conditioning predictor has the highest impact on price with a coefficient of 0.857

The negative coefficient for the age predictor seems coherent with the idea, that the older the lower the price of it. Same reasoning holds for the negative relationship mileage has with price. The air conditioning brings the price up by the “largest” factor, given the data is standardized and therefore comparable.

The variable Airco, doesn’t show up as significant, maybe for redundancy with the Automatic_airco, This could be a good candidate for dropping of variables.

We run an exhaustive search, meaning we evaluate possible predictors subsets, in this case we are looking over a set of 16 predictors, ergo we evaluate 16 models. If we observe regular R^2, so the percentage of variation explained by the predictors increases steadily as we include more and more variables. Adjusted R^2 adds a penalty for every new variable, therefore accounting for the bias-variance trade off we are currently leading. In this case R^2 adj seems to be behaving basically the same way regular R^2 does, meaning that all these variables seem to increase the portion of variation described by the model.
